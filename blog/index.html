SpARCL: Specificity-Aware Representations for Contrastive Learning

Abinitha Gourabathina, Aarush Gupta, Rayna Arora

Introduction and Motivation

Contrastive learning is a self-supervised technique that learns meaningful representations by structuring the representation space based on similarities and dissimilarities in the data. The method constructs “positive”, or similar, pairs of inputs and “negative”, or dissimilar, pairs of inputs to define a learning objective. The goal is to minimize the distance between positive pairs and maximize the distance between negative pairs in the embedding space.

Recent research in contrastive learning has explored the impact of how positive and negative pairs are chosen, even focusing on pairing instances with finer-grained shared attributes. Tian et al. (2020) study how different augmentations affect the similarity of positive pairs, showing that higher specificity in positive pairs between individual image augmentations can lead to better performance particularly when the added specificity is task-relevant [5]. Zhu et al. (2021) explore using synthesized variations of an image as positive pairs, suggesting that varying the positive pair specificity can help create richer, more transferable embeddings [6]. Both of these works specifically examine unsupervised contrastive learning contexts where positive pairs are created in the image setting by augmenting or varying an existing image.

Within supervised contrastive learning, approaches by Khosla et al. (2020) have focused on pairing positive instances by exact class labels [4]. Extending this work, we leverage a hierarchical label structure to vary the specificity of positive pairs within supervised contrastive learning. We evaluate the quality of these representations on a fixed classification task, where the positive classes for the contrastive learning scheme may be more or less specific than the fixed classification labels. This study seeks to answer two key questions: (1) Does increasing the specificity of positive pairs lead to a more task-relevant and discriminative representation space? (2) What trade-offs exist between improved discrimination and generalizability when using finer-grained positive pairs? The answers to these questions are not immediately obvious without experimental results: for example, while fine-grained positive classes could increase structure in the representation space, it can also be argued that the representation space could be overfit to very specific classes in the presence of limited data. Through these investigations, we aim to provide insights into the role of granularity in shaping contrastive learning outcomes and contribute to the broader understanding of representation learning techniques.

In carrying out this study, we also employ a novel contrastive training scheme distinct from the binary positive-negative pairing framework commonly employed in contrastive learning (Chen et al., 2020) [1]. Traditional contrastive methods often oversimplify relationships between data points, treating them as entirely similar or dissimilar. Works exploring soft contrastive learning and hierarchical embeddings (Vendrov et al., 2016) highlight the importance of incorporating continuous similarity measures into representation learning [4, 7].

Our approach introduces varying degrees of similarity, defined by a distance metric between classes derived from the hierarchical structure of the dataset. These distances weight the contrastive loss, allowing the model to dynamically adjust its sensitivity to different similarity levels and learn more meaningful representations. For instance, labels further apart in the hierarchy are considered less similar and are weighted less in the contrastive loss, while closer labels are weighted more in the loss (i.e. more “positive”). This flexible weighting scheme provides a more nuanced framework for contrastive learning on inputs with non-binary similarity relations.

Dataset

Given the hierarchical nature of the problem, we conducted experiments using the ImageNet dataset, which contains over ten million images (Deng et al., 2009) [2]. Each image is associated with one or more synonym sets, or “synsets,” representing classes of objects or general concepts. These synsets are organized hierarchically in a tree structure, enabling our contrastive learning approach to leverage labels at varying levels of specificity within the hierarchy. This hierarchical labeling system is central to testing our hypotheses.

For our experiments, we focused on a subset of the hierarchy consisting of dog images labeled at different levels of specificity (e.g., general categories, specific breeds). This subset was chosen because classifying different dog breeds through images presents a particularly challenging task, even for humans. Such a difficult classification problem highlights performance differences more effectively, making it ideal for our analysis. Limiting the scope to dog images also reduced the dataset size, which was critical for running experiments efficiently given our computational constraints. The figure below demonstrates the “sporting dog” subtree of the hierarchical dataset, which contains the labels “retriever,” “spaniel,” “setter,” and “pointer,” each of which contains multiple class labels. There are many such subtrees under the “dog” umbrella.

Methods

To investigate our hypotheses, we considered the classification problem at a fixed specificity level. Specifically, we chose to use classification labels sporting dog, terrier, and hound for our primary classification task. Within this framework, we vary the specificity of the positive classes in the contrastive learning scheme — the various levels are visualized in the table below.

In the contrastive learning scheme, the NCE loss is applied to values in an intermediate “embedding” layer of the overall architecture, which serves as the representation space. Then, the standard contrastive loss formulation for positive pairs produced through data augmentation is

Lcontr=∑t∈D[−log⁡(exp⁡(sim(zt,zt′)/τ)∑k=1nexp⁡(sim(zt,zk)/τ))]

where t′ is the augmented sample, zi is the embedding for input i, and sim is the standard cosine similarity formula

sim(u→,v→)=u→⋅v→||u→||||v→||.

However, a unique aspect of our study is that some negative pairs are “more different” than others, depending on their positions in the tree structure. As mentioned earlier, we thus use a contrastive loss weighted with a distance measure. This distance is calculated through the least common ancestor (LCA) distance between two nodes in the tree, given by

dLCA(u,v)=min(depth(u)−depth(ℓ),depth(v)−depth(ℓ)),

where ℓ=LCA(u,v) in the tree structure.

A naive approach to measure class dissimilarity might involve using the raw path length between two nodes. However, this approach has significant limitations. For example, a class with greater specificity on one side of the tree would disproportionately increase the distance to another class. However, this is not necessarily true — for example, this logic would imply that a retriever in the below tree is more similar to a setter than to a gordon setter, solely because the latter belongs to a more specific class. While we do assume that higher specificity in both classes implies greater dissimilarity—since greater specificity suggests stronger distinguishing characteristics between the classes—our LCA distance measure avoids penalizing asymmetry in the specificity levels of the compared classes. The below figure illustrates this concept, showing how the LCA distance represents the relationships between classes.

Figure 2. A subtree of the overall label hierarchy for the dog classification tasks. The LCA distance calculation between two pairs of labels is visualized.

Then, the model is trained with contrastive loss

Lcontr′=∑t∈D∑f∈D[−log⁡(exp⁡(sim(zt,zf))∑k=1nexp⁡(sim(zt,zk)))⋅f(distLCA(yt,yf))]

where zi is the vector embedding for example i, yi is the class label for example i, and sim(u,v) is the cosine similarity of vectors u, v.

Note that in our contrastive loss, we weight by a function f of the LCA distance, which allows fine-grained control of how much the tree distance influence the contrastive loss. This formulation allows us to vary the importance of hierarchical dissimilarities in shaping the embedding space while maintaining the balance between classification accuracy and the contrastive objective. In this study, we consider

f(x)∈{y=1log⁡(1+x),11+x,e−x,5−x}.

Note that all functions are decreasing over the positive integers, which implies lower weighting of the positive contrastive term for labels at higher distances, as desired.

By weighting negative examples in this way, the model learns embeddings that are not only informed by pairwise dissimilarities but are also grounded in the hierarchical relationships inherent to the data. This approach helps improve the representation of classes within the embedding space and aligns with our overarching goal of leveraging hierarchical specificity to improve classification performance.

We encountered challenges during training when the contrastive loss significantly exceeded the classification loss, likely causing discrepancies in the training speed of each model. As these losses were calculated through entirely different methods, no parameter-based normalization approach could be rationally justified. This issue was addressed by reweighting the contrastive loss using the ratio of classification loss to contrastive loss calculated from the first batch of the initial training epoch.

Model Architecture and Setup

For these experiments, we primarily use the pretrained ResNet18 model, a convolutional neural network with residual connections, or direct “skip” connections between non-adjacent layers — this model is relatively powerful which allows for sufficient performance on classification problems while also being compute-efficient, only consisting of 18 layers. The ResNet18 maps inputs into the representation or “embedding” space, where the contrastive loss is applied. Then, a simple two-layer MLP (multi-layer perceptron) with a final softmax activation is used for the classification output, on which the standard cross-entropy classification loss is applied.

Figure 3. Model architecture of contrasting training scheme. The pre-trained ResNet-18 model first maps the input images into a representation space, which is used to compute the contrastive loss Lcontr across all samples in a batch using the weighting scheme previously mentioned. This is added to the standard classification loss Lclf (e.g. cross-entropy loss) to get the total loss function on which backpropagation is performed.

Increasing the specificity of representations in contrastive learning has a notable impact on downstream performance and the model’s ability to generalize. Our findings demonstrate that specificity level 2 consistently delivers strong performance, particularly for the logarithmic and linear distance functions (see Figure 4). Interestingly, specificity 2 aligns with the granularity required for the classification task, striking a balance between overfitting at specificity 3 and insufficient representation at lower specificity levels. This suggests that specificity 2 achieves an optimal level of granularity for effective generalization.

Performance also varies across distance functions.

  • Log-Reciprocal, y=1log⁡(1+x): Specificity 2 yields the highest validation accuracy (74.13%) and balanced precision, recall, and F1 scores. While performance remains strong at specificity 3 (accuracy: 71.92%), it does not surpass results at specificity 2, solidifying specificity 2 as the optimal choice.
  • Linear, y=5−x: Performance peaks at specificity 2, achieving optimal accuracy (71.73%) and strong metrics across the board. However, specificity 3 results in a noticeable performance drop, underscoring the importance of moderate specificity levels for generalization.
  • Reciprocal, y=11+x: Performance lags significantly across all specificity levels, with a best accuracy of 71.23%. Furthermore, validation accuracies for the reciprocal function exhibit instability, with steep declines from specificity 0 to 1 and from specificity 2 to 3. This instability highlights its limited effectiveness.
  • Exponential, y=e−x: Highest accuracy (71.2%) is observed at specificity 0, with performance gradually decreasing as specificity increases. Improvement beyond specificity 2 remains minimal.Overall, specificity 2 emerges as the preferred choice for achieving optimal accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 scores, particularly when paired with the log distance function, which consistently leads to the best-performing models.

To better understand the relationship between specificity and the embedding space properties, we analyzed the diameter and margin of the embedding spaces produced by the models. Our initial hypothesis posited that optimal models would exhibit the highest margin and lowest diameter, indicative of compact representations and clear class separation.

Specificity 2 consistently results in the lowest diameter for models using the same distance function, corresponding to its superior performance (see Figure). Models employing the log-reciprocal function exhibit both the lowest diameter and the highest classification performance. However, across different distance functions, no consistent trend emerges between diameter and performance. This suggests that the relationship between diameter and performance may be influenced by the mathematical properties of the distance function rather than the compactness of representations.

No discernible trend is observed between margin and classification performance. While we hypothesized that higher margins would correspond to better separation, our results suggest that additional factors may contribute to performance. Future studies could refine these findings by examining embedding space density and calculating diameter and margin relative to the overall embedding space size. This would enable more accurate comparisons between models and further elucidate the relationship between embedding space properties and performance.

Conclusion

Summary of Results

Our findings reveal that a contrastive specificity level of 2 consistently delivers strong performance, particularly when paired with the log-reciprocal and log-linear distance functions. This aligns with the underlying classification task’s specificity level, suggesting that increasing the specificity of contrastive representations beyond this point may hinder performance by directing the model’s focus toward classifying “irrelevant” features. Conversely, lower specificity levels fail to capture the necessary information for effective classification, resulting in degraded performance. Notably, the limited performance of higher specificity levels may also reflect the constraints of our computational resources, as fine-grained representations likely require more training epochs to fully develop.

The log-reciprocal function emerged as a robust choice, demonstrating strong accuracy and achieving a low diameter. However, our results do not conclusively clarify the relationship between classification accuracy and embedding quality, as measured by diameter and margin. Since these metrics are specific to the distance function used, normalization is likely necessary to enable accurate comparisons across functions.

Limitations and Future Work

Certain limitations of this study stem from the dataset and hierarchical structure used. Our focus was domain-specific, concentrating on images of dogs, which raises questions about the generalizability of these methods to other types of images. This focus was dictated by the sparse and inconsistent branching of other ImageNet categories. Additionally, deeper hierarchical structures could have provided more insights across a broader range of specificity levels — the hierarchical tree also did not optimally capture visually observable differences such as color, fur patterns, or face shape, which may have been better suited for an image-based classification task.

Addressing these limitations in future work is critical. The poor performance observed at higher specificity levels may stem from insufficient training epochs, constrained by the need to conduct multiple tests. Extended training time might allow these complex representations to surpass the performance of those at specificity level 2. More comprehensive hyperparameter tuning could also enhance performance, particularly for distance functions that underperformed in this study.

Despite these challenges, this work underscores the importance of contrastive specificity in shaping representation quality and downstream classification outcomes, while also suggesting a novel loss function and contrastive training scheme for problems where similarity is not a binary metric. The idea of non-binary similarity is applicable to any problem where there is a notion of distance between samples, even without a hierarchical data structure. Future research is needed to apply a similar distance-based contrastive loss approach to other problems as well as compare it to the standard 0-1 contrastive loss function, providing deeper insights into the efficacy and limitations of this method. All in all, this work and its future extensions are crucial for better understanding contrastive training in the fine-grained setting, both for specificity of class-pairing and for fine-grained similarity weighting of contrastive losses.
